## PART A: ISO/IEC 42001:2023 — AI Management System (AIMS) Lead Auditor

---

### ✅ Step 1: Understood the ISO/IEC 42001:2023 Standard

- Studied the world's **first international standard for AI Management Systems (AIMS)**
- Understood the standard's purpose:
  - Provide a framework for organizations that **develop, provide, or use AI systems**
  - Ensure AI is deployed **responsibly, ethically, transparently, and with appropriate controls**
  - Establish a **management system approach** to AI governance — similar to ISO 27001 for information security
- Studied the standard's structure:
  - Clauses 4–10 (Management System Requirements)
  - Annex A (AI Controls)
  - Annex B (AI Risk Sources)
  - Annex C (AI Objectives)
  - Annex D (AI Domain and Sector Considerations)

---

### ✅ Step 2: Learned the AIMS Management System Framework

- Studied the **management system clauses** (4–10):

| Clause | Topic | Key Content |
|--------|-------|-------------|
| **4** | Context of the Organization | Understanding the organization, interested parties, AIMS scope, AI policy |
| **5** | Leadership | Top management commitment, AI policy, roles and responsibilities |
| **6** | Planning | AI risk assessment, AI objectives, planning for changes |
| **7** | Support | Resources, competence, awareness, communication, documented information |
| **8** | Operation | Operational planning, AI risk treatment, AI system impact assessment |
| **9** | Performance Evaluation | Monitoring, measurement, internal audit, management review |
| **10** | Improvement | Nonconformity, corrective action, continual improvement |

## PART B: AI Security and Governance

---

### ✅ Step 6: Studied AI-Specific Security Threats

- Learned about security threats unique to AI systems:

| Threat | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Adversarial Attacks** | Manipulating input data to fool AI models into making wrong predictions |
| **Data Poisoning** | Injecting malicious data into training datasets to corrupt model behavior |
| **Model Theft** | Extracting or reverse-engineering AI models to steal intellectual property |
| **Model Inversion** | Using model outputs to reconstruct sensitive training data |
| **Evasion Attacks** | Crafting inputs that bypass AI-based security systems (e.g., malware detection) |
| **Prompt Injection** | Manipulating prompts in generative AI to bypass safety controls |
| **Supply Chain Attacks** | Compromising third-party AI components, libraries, or pre-trained models |

- Understood how these threats differ from traditional cybersecurity threats
- Learned defensive strategies for each threat category

---

### ✅ Step 7: Learned AI Governance Frameworks

- Studied multiple **AI governance frameworks** beyond ISO 42001:

| Framework | Source | Focus |
|-----------|--------|-------|
| **NIST AI RMF** | US National Institute of Standards & Technology | AI risk management — Govern, Map, Measure, Manage |
| **EU AI Act** | European Union | Risk-based AI regulation — Unacceptable, High, Limited, Minimal risk |
| **OECD AI Principles** | Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development | Trustworthy AI — transparency, accountability, human-centered values |
| **UNESCO AI Ethics** | United Nations | Ethical AI — fairness, sustainability, proportionality |

- Compared frameworks to understand **common themes**:
  - transparency and explainability
  - fairness and non-discrimination
  - human oversight
  - accountability
  - privacy and data protection
  - safety and security
  - environmental sustainability
